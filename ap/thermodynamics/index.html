    Thermodynamics¶

    Explanation¶

    Thermodynamics studies energy transformations and predicts reaction spontaneity. Understanding entropy and Gibbs free energy is essential for AP Chemistry and predicting chemical behavior.

    Key Concepts¶

    Laws of Thermodynamics: - First Law: Energy is conserved. \(\Delta E = q + w\) - \(\Delta E\) = change in internal energy - \(q\) = heat (+ absorbed, - released) - \(w\) = work (+ done on system, - done by system)

    Enthalpy (\(H\)): Heat content at constant pressure - \(\Delta H = q_p\) (heat at constant pressure) - Hess's Law: \(\Delta H\) is path-independent

    Entropy (\(S\)): Measure of disorder/randomness - Units: J/molÂ·K - Higher entropy = more disorder - \(\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}}\)

    Entropy Changes: - Increase entropy (\(\Delta S > 0\)): - Gas formation from liquid/solid - More moles of gas products than reactants - Dissolving, mixing, heating - Decrease entropy (\(\Delta S < 0\)): - Gas â†’ liquid/solid - Fewer moles of gas products - Cooling, ordering

    Calculating \(\Delta SÂ°\): [\Delta SÂ° = \sum SÂ°{\text{products}} - \sum SÂ°]}

    Second Law: Spontaneous processes increase universal entropy (\(\Delta S_{\text{univ}} > 0\))

    Third Law: Perfect crystal at 0 K has \(S = 0\)

    Gibbs Free Energy (\(G\)): Predicts spontaneity at constant T and P [\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S]

    Spontaneity: - \(\Delta G < 0\): Spontaneous (thermodynamically favored) - \(\Delta G > 0\): Non-spontaneous (thermodynamically unfavored) - \(\Delta G = 0\): At equilibrium

    Temperature Dependence:

    \(\Delta H\) \(\Delta S\) \(\Delta G\) Spontaneity
    - (exo) + (increase) Always - Spontaneous at all T
    + (endo) - (decrease) Always + Never spontaneous
    - (exo) - (decrease) - at low T Spontaneous at low T
    + (endo) + (increase) - at high T Spontaneous at high T

    Standard Free Energy: [\Delta GÂ° = \sum \Delta G_fÂ°(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta G_fÂ°(\text{reactants})] [\Delta GÂ° = -RT\ln K] - \(R = 8.314 \text{ J/molÂ·K}\) - \(K\) = equilibrium constant

    Relationship to Equilibrium: [\Delta G = \Delta GÂ° + RT\ln Q]

    Enhanced Explanations¶

    Understanding Entropy Step-by-Step¶

    What is Entropy? Entropy (\(S\)) measures the number of possible microscopic arrangements (microstates) of a system. More microstates mean higher entropy and more disorder.

    Step-by-Step Approach to Entropy:

    1. Identify the initial and final states: What physical or chemical change is occurring?

    2. Consider molecular freedom: More freedom of movement means higher entropy

    3. Gases have the highest entropy (molecules move freely in all directions)
    4. Liquids have intermediate entropy (molecules can slide past each other)

    5. Solids have the lowest entropy (molecules are fixed in position)

    6. Count moles of gas: Gas phase has the biggest impact on entropy

    7. More moles of gas = higher entropy

    8. Compare total moles of gas on each side of equation

    9. Consider temperature: Higher temperature always increases entropy

    10. More kinetic energy = more possible arrangements

    11. Calculate \(\Delta SÂ°\) using standard molar entropies: [\Delta SÂ° = \sum n SÂ°{\text{products}} - \sum m SÂ°] where }\(n\) and \(m\) are stoichiometric coefficients

    Example: For \(N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightarrow 2NH_3(g)\) - Reactants: 4 moles of gas (1 + 3) - Products: 2 moles of gas - \(\Delta SÂ° < 0\) (entropy decreases) because we have fewer moles of gas

    Understanding Gibbs Free Energy Step-by-Step¶

    What is Gibbs Free Energy? Gibbs free energy (\(G\)) combines enthalpy and entropy to predict spontaneity at constant temperature and pressure. It represents the maximum useful work obtainable from a process.

    Step-by-Step Approach to Gibbs Free Energy:

    1. Identify \(\Delta H\): Is the reaction exothermic (â€“) or endothermic (+)?
    2. Exothermic releases heat, favoring spontaneity

    3. Endothermic absorbs heat, opposing spontaneity

    4. Identify \(\Delta S\): Does entropy increase (+) or decrease (â€“)?

    5. Entropy increase favors spontaneity

    6. Entropy decrease opposes spontaneity

    7. Check units: CRITICAL for avoiding errors

    8. \(\Delta H\) is typically in kJ or kJ/mol
    9. \(\Delta S\) is typically in J/K or J/molÂ·K

    10. Convert \(\Delta S\) to kJ/K by dividing by 1000

    11. Apply the Gibbs equation: [\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S]

    12. \(T\) must be in Kelvin (K = Â°C + 273.15)

    13. All terms must have consistent units

    14. Interpret the result:

    15. \(\Delta G < 0\): Spontaneous (reaction favors products)
    16. \(\Delta G > 0\): Non-spontaneous (reaction favors reactants)
    17. \(\Delta G = 0\): System at equilibrium

    Example Calculation: Given: \(\Delta HÂ° = -200 \text{ kJ}\), \(\Delta SÂ° = +150 \text{ J/K}\), \(T = 298 \text{ K}\)

    • Convert: \(\Delta SÂ° = 150 \text{ J/K} = 0.150 \text{ kJ/K}\)
    • Calculate: \(\Delta GÂ° = -200 - (298)(0.150)\)
    • \(\Delta GÂ° = -200 - 44.7 = -244.7 \text{ kJ}\)
    • Result: Spontaneous (both enthalpy and entropy favor the reaction)

    Understanding Spontaneity and Temperature¶

    How Temperature Affects Spontaneity:

    The equation \(\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S\) shows that temperature can change whether a reaction is spontaneous.

    Four Scenarios:

    1. \(\Delta H < 0\), \(\Delta S > 0\): Spontaneous at ALL temperatures

    2. Both terms favor spontaneity: \(\Delta G = (-) - T(+) = (-) - (+) = \text{always negative}\)

    3. \(\Delta H > 0\), \(\Delta S < 0\): NEVER spontaneous

    4. Both terms oppose spontaneity: \(\Delta G = (+) - T(-) = (+) + (+) = \text{always positive}\)

    5. \(\Delta H < 0\), \(\Delta S < 0\): Spontaneous at LOW temperatures

    6. Enthalpy favors, entropy opposes
    7. At low T: \(|\Delta H| > T|\Delta S|\), so \(\Delta G < 0\)

    8. At high T: \(T|\Delta S| > |\Delta H|\), so \(\Delta G > 0\)

    9. \(\Delta H > 0\), \(\Delta S > 0\): Spontaneous at HIGH temperatures

    10. Entropy favors, enthalpy opposes
    11. At low T: \(\Delta H > T\Delta S\), so \(\Delta G > 0\)
    12. At high T: \(T\Delta S > \Delta H\), so \(\Delta G < 0\)

    Finding the Crossover Temperature: Set \(\Delta G = 0\) and solve for T: [T = \frac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}] This is the temperature where the reaction transitions between spontaneous and non-spontaneous.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid¶

    Mistake 1: Sign Convention Errors with \(\Delta S\)¶

    Error: Forgetting that in \(\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S\), the \(\Delta S\) term is SUBTRACTED.

    Example of Wrong Thinking: "Since \(\Delta S\) is negative and we're subtracting it, that makes \(\Delta G\) more negative, favoring spontaneity."

    Correct Analysis: \(\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S = \Delta H - T(-\Delta S) = \Delta H + T|\Delta S|\)

    A negative \(\Delta S\) actually INCREASES \(\Delta G\) (makes it more positive), opposing spontaneity.

    How to Avoid: Always substitute actual values with signs, then do the arithmetic carefully.

    Mistake 2: Unit Conversion for \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta S\)¶

    Error: Using \(\Delta H\) in kJ and \(\Delta S\) in J/K without converting to the same unit.

    Wrong Calculation: \(\Delta H = -50 \text{ kJ}\), \(\Delta S = 100 \text{ J/K}\), \(T = 300 \text{ K}\) \(\Delta G = -50 - (300)(100) = -50 - 30000 = -30050 \text{ kJ}\) (WRONG!)

    Correct Calculation: Convert \(\Delta S = 100 \text{ J/K} = 0.100 \text{ kJ/K}\) \(\Delta G = -50 - (300)(0.100) = -50 - 30 = -80 \text{ kJ}\) (CORRECT)

    How to Avoid: ALWAYS convert \(\Delta S\) from J/K to kJ/K (divide by 1000) before plugging into the equation.

    Mistake 3: Confusing \(\Delta G\) and \(\Delta GÂ°\)¶

    Error: Thinking \(\Delta GÂ°\) and \(\Delta G\) are the same thing.

    Key Differences: - \(\Delta GÂ°\): Standard free energy change (all species at 1 M, 1 atm, 25Â°C) - \(\Delta G\): Free energy change under ANY conditions

    Relationship: [\Delta G = \Delta GÂ° + RT\ln Q]

    When to Use Which: - Use \(\Delta GÂ°\) to find equilibrium constant: \(\Delta GÂ° = -RT\ln K\) - Use \(\Delta G\) to determine if a reaction is spontaneous under specific conditions - At equilibrium: \(Q = K\) and \(\Delta G = 0\) (but \(\Delta GÂ°\) may not be zero!)

    How to Avoid: Always check whether you're working with standard conditions or actual conditions.

    Mistake 4: Temperature Dependence Assumptions¶

    Error: Assuming \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta S\) change significantly with temperature.

    Reality: For most AP Chemistry problems, \(\Delta HÂ°\) and \(\Delta SÂ°\) are approximately constant over moderate temperature ranges (room temperature to a few hundred degrees).

    This allows us to write: [\Delta G(T) = \Delta HÂ° - T\Delta SÂ°] even at temperatures other than 25Â°C.

    When this breaks down: At very high temperatures (above 1000 K) or involving phase changes, \(\Delta H\) and \(\Delta S\) can change.

    How to Avoid: Unless told otherwise, treat \(\Delta HÂ°\) and \(\Delta SÂ°\) as constants when calculating \(\Delta G\) at different temperatures.

    Mistake 5: Misinterpreting Spontaneity and Reaction Rate¶

    Error: Thinking \(\Delta G < 0\) means the reaction occurs quickly.

    Reality: - Thermodynamics (Gibbs free energy) tells us IF a reaction will occur (direction) - Kinetics (activation energy) tells us HOW FAST it occurs (rate)

    Classic Example: Diamond converting to graphite - \(\Delta G < 0\) at room temperature (thermodynamically favored) - But the reaction is essentially infinitely slow (high activation energy) - Diamonds are "forever" kinetically, not thermodynamically

    Another Example: Combustion of gasoline and oxygen - Very negative \(\Delta G\) (highly spontaneous) - But doesn't occur until you provide a spark (activation energy)

    How to Avoid: Remember that spontaneity only indicates the final equilibrium position, not the path or speed to get there.
