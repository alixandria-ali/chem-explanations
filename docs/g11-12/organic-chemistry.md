## Explanation

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds. Carbon's unique ability to form four covalent bonds and bond with itself creates an enormous variety of molecules, from simple methane to complex DNA.

### Key Concepts

#### Why Carbon is Special

**Carbon's unique properties**:
- **4 valence electrons**: Can form 4 covalent bonds
- **Catenation**: Ability to form long chains and rings with itself
- **Versatile bonding**: Can form single, double, and triple bonds
- **Forms stable bonds**: With hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, halogens, and other carbons

This allows for millions of different organic compounds with diverse properties and functions.

### Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons are compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen.

#### Alkanes (Saturated Hydrocarbons)

**General formula**: \(C_nH_{2n+2}\)

**Characteristics**:
- All single bonds (C-C and C-H)
- Saturated (maximum number of hydrogens)
- Suffix: **-ane**

**Common alkanes**:
- Methane: \(CH_4\)
- Ethane: \(C_2H_6\)
- Propane: \(C_3H_8\)
- Butane: \(C_4H_{10}\)
- Pentane: \(C_5H_{12}\)
- Hexane: \(C_6H_{14}\)
- Heptane: \(C_7H_{16}\)
- Octane: \(C_8H_{18}\)
- Nonane: \(C_9H_{20}\)
- Decane: \(C_{10}H_{22}\)

**Naming prefix memory aid**:
- **Meth-** = 1 carbon
- **Eth-** = 2 carbons
- **Prop-** = 3 carbons
- **But-** = 4 carbons
- **Pent-** = 5 carbons
- **Hex-** = 6 carbons
- **Hept-** = 7 carbons
- **Oct-** = 8 carbons
- **Non-** = 9 carbons
- **Dec-** = 10 carbons

#### Alkenes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)

**General formula**: \(C_nH_{2n}\)

**Characteristics**:
- Contain at least one C=C double bond
- Unsaturated (fewer hydrogens than alkanes)
- Suffix: **-ene**
- More reactive than alkanes

**Examples**:
- Ethene (ethylene): \(C_2H_4\)
- Propene: \(C_3H_6\)
- Butene: \(C_4H_8\)

**Naming**: Number the chain so the double bond gets the lowest number.
- Example: But-1-ene vs. But-2-ene

#### Alkynes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)

**General formula**: \(C_nH_{2n-2}\)

**Characteristics**:
- Contain at least one C≡C triple bond
- Unsaturated (even fewer hydrogens)
- Suffix: **-yne**
- Highly reactive

**Examples**:
- Ethyne (acetylene): \(C_2H_2\)
- Propyne: \(C_3H_4\)
- Butyne: \(C_4H_6\)

#### Cyclic Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons that form ring structures.

**Examples**:
- Cyclopropane: \(C_3H_6\) (3-carbon ring)
- Cyclohexane: \(C_6H_{12}\) (6-carbon ring)
- Benzene: \(C_6H_6\) (aromatic ring with delocalized electrons)

**Naming**: Add prefix **cyclo-** before the alkane name.

### IUPAC Nomenclature Rules

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) system provides systematic names for organic compounds.

#### Step-by-Step Naming Process

**Step 1: Find the longest carbon chain** (parent chain)
- This determines the base name (meth-, eth-, prop-, etc.)

**Step 2: Identify and name substituents** (branches)
- Alkyl groups: Remove one H from alkane, change -ane to -yl
  - Methyl: \(-CH_3\)
  - Ethyl: \(-C_2H_5\)
  - Propyl: \(-C_3H_7\)
  - Butyl: \(-C_4H_9\)

**Step 3: Number the carbon chain**
- Number from the end that gives substituents the lowest numbers
- For alkenes/alkynes, prioritize giving the multiple bond the lowest number

**Step 4: Write the name**
- Format: (position)-(substituent)-(parent name)
- Multiple substituents: List alphabetically
- Same substituent appears multiple times: Use prefixes di-, tri-, tetra-
- Example: 2,3-dimethylbutane

#### Examples of Naming

**Example 1**:
```
    CH₃
     |
CH₃-CH-CH₂-CH₃
```
- Longest chain: 4 carbons (butane)
- Substituent: Methyl group on carbon 2
- Name: **2-methylbutane**

**Example 2**:
```
    CH₃  CH₃
     |    |
CH₃-CH-CH-CH₃
```
- Longest chain: 4 carbons (butane)
- Substituents: Two methyl groups on carbons 2 and 3
- Name: **2,3-dimethylbutane**

### Functional Groups

Functional groups are specific atoms or groups of atoms that give organic molecules characteristic properties and reactivity.

#### Major Functional Groups

| Functional Group | Structure | Suffix/Prefix | Example |
|-----------------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Alkane** | C-C | -ane | Ethane |
| **Alkene** | C=C | -ene | Ethene |
| **Alkyne** | C≡C | -yne | Ethyne |
| **Alcohol** | -OH | -ol | Ethanol |
| **Aldehyde** | -CHO | -al | Ethanal |
| **Ketone** | -CO- | -one | Propanone |
| **Carboxylic Acid** | -COOH | -oic acid | Ethanoic acid |
| **Ester** | -COO- | -oate | Methyl ethanoate |
| **Ether** | -O- | ether | Diethyl ether |
| **Amine** | -NH₂ | -amine | Ethylamine |
| **Halide** | -X (F, Cl, Br, I) | halo- | Chloroethane |

#### Alcohols

**Structure**: Contains -OH (hydroxyl) group
**General formula**: \(R-OH\) where R is an alkyl group
**Suffix**: -ol

**Classification**:
- **Primary (1°)**: -OH on a carbon bonded to one other carbon
  - Example: Ethanol \(CH_3CH_2OH\)
- **Secondary (2°)**: -OH on a carbon bonded to two other carbons
  - Example: Propan-2-ol \(CH_3CH(OH)CH_3\)
- **Tertiary (3°)**: -OH on a carbon bonded to three other carbons

**Naming**: Number the chain to give the -OH the lowest number.
- Example: Butan-2-ol (not butan-3-ol)

#### Aldehydes and Ketones

Both contain the carbonyl group (C=O), but differ in position.

**Aldehydes**:
- C=O at the **end** of the chain
- General structure: \(R-CHO\)
- Suffix: -al
- Example: Ethanal (acetaldehyde) \(CH_3CHO\)

**Ketones**:
- C=O in the **middle** of the chain
- General structure: \(R-CO-R'\)
- Suffix: -one
- Example: Propanone (acetone) \(CH_3COCH_3\)

#### Carboxylic Acids

**Structure**: Contains -COOH (carboxyl) group
**General formula**: \(R-COOH\)
**Suffix**: -oic acid

**Properties**:
- Acidic (can donate H⁺)
- Form hydrogen bonds (high boiling points)

**Examples**:
- Methanoic acid (formic acid): \(HCOOH\)
- Ethanoic acid (acetic acid): \(CH_3COOH\)
- Propanoic acid: \(CH_3CH_2COOH\)

#### Esters

**Structure**: \(R-COO-R'\)
**Formed from**: Carboxylic acid + Alcohol
**Naming**: (alkyl from alcohol) (alkanoate from acid)

**Example**:
- \(CH_3COOCH_2CH_3\) = Ethyl ethanoate
- From ethanoic acid + ethanol

**Properties**: Pleasant, fruity smells (used in perfumes and flavorings)

### Isomerism

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structures or arrangements.

#### Structural (Constitutional) Isomers

Same molecular formula, different connectivity of atoms.

**Types**:

**1. Chain isomers**: Different carbon chain arrangements
- Example: Butane \(C_4H_{10}\)
  - n-Butane: \(CH_3-CH_2-CH_2-CH_3\) (straight chain)
  - 2-Methylpropane: \(CH_3-CH(CH_3)-CH_3\) (branched)

**2. Position isomers**: Same functional group, different position
- Example: Propanol \(C_3H_7OH\)
  - Propan-1-ol: \(CH_3CH_2CH_2OH\)
  - Propan-2-ol: \(CH_3CH(OH)CH_3\)

**3. Functional group isomers**: Different functional groups
- Example: \(C_2H_6O\)
  - Ethanol: \(CH_3CH_2OH\) (alcohol)
  - Methoxymethane: \(CH_3OCH_3\) (ether)

#### Geometric (Cis-Trans) Isomers

Occur in alkenes due to restricted rotation around C=C double bond.

**Cis isomer**: Same groups on the same side of the double bond
**Trans isomer**: Same groups on opposite sides of the double bond

**Example**: But-2-ene
- **Cis-but-2-ene**: Both CH₃ groups on the same side
- **Trans-but-2-ene**: CH₃ groups on opposite sides

**Requirement**: Each carbon of the double bond must have two different groups.

### Types of Organic Reactions

#### 1. Combustion

**Definition**: Reaction with oxygen to produce CO₂ and H₂O, releasing energy

**Complete combustion**:
\[\text{Hydrocarbon} + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O + \text{energy}\]

**Example**:
\[CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O\]

**Incomplete combustion** (insufficient oxygen):
- Produces CO (carbon monoxide) or C (soot) instead of CO₂
- Example: \(2CH_4 + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2CO + 4H_2O\)

#### 2. Addition Reactions

**Definition**: Two molecules combine to form one product (breaking a multiple bond)

**Common in**: Alkenes and alkynes

**Examples**:

**Hydrogenation** (adding H₂):
\[C_2H_4 + H_2 \rightarrow C_2H_6\]
\[\text{Ethene} + \text{Hydrogen} \rightarrow \text{Ethane}\]

**Halogenation** (adding X₂ like Br₂ or Cl₂):
\[C_2H_4 + Br_2 \rightarrow C_2H_4Br_2\]
\[\text{Ethene} + \text{Bromine} \rightarrow \text{1,2-dibromoethane}\]

**Test for alkenes**: Bromine water (brown) decolorizes when added to an alkene.

**Hydration** (adding H₂O):
\[C_2H_4 + H_2O \rightarrow C_2H_5OH\]
\[\text{Ethene} + \text{Water} \rightarrow \text{Ethanol}\]

#### 3. Substitution Reactions

**Definition**: One atom or group replaces another in a molecule

**Common in**: Alkanes, alcohols, halogenoalkanes

**Example - Halogenation of alkanes** (with UV light):
\[CH_4 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{UV} CH_3Cl + HCl\]
\[\text{Methane} + \text{Chlorine} \rightarrow \text{Chloromethane} + \text{Hydrogen chloride}\]

#### 4. Elimination Reactions

**Definition**: Removal of atoms from a molecule to form a double bond

**Common reaction**: Dehydration of alcohols (removing H₂O)

**Example**:
\[C_2H_5OH \xrightarrow{\text{conc. } H_2SO_4, \text{ heat}} C_2H_4 + H_2O\]
\[\text{Ethanol} \rightarrow \text{Ethene} + \text{Water}\]

#### 5. Esterification

**Definition**: Formation of an ester from carboxylic acid and alcohol

**General reaction**:
\[\text{Carboxylic acid} + \text{Alcohol} \xrightarrow{H^+ \text{ catalyst}} \text{Ester} + \text{Water}\]

**Example**:
\[CH_3COOH + C_2H_5OH \rightleftharpoons CH_3COOC_2H_5 + H_2O\]
\[\text{Ethanoic acid} + \text{Ethanol} \rightleftharpoons \text{Ethyl ethanoate} + \text{Water}\]

**Conditions**: Acid catalyst (usually conc. H₂SO₄), heat

### Physical Properties and Trends

#### Boiling Points

**Factors affecting boiling point**:

**1. Chain length**: Longer chains → higher boiling point
- More surface area → stronger London dispersion forces
- Example: Methane (bp: -162°C) < Ethane (bp: -89°C) < Propane (bp: -42°C)

**2. Branching**: More branching → lower boiling point
- Less surface area for intermolecular forces
- Example: n-Butane (bp: -0.5°C) > 2-Methylpropane (bp: -11.7°C)

**3. Functional groups**:
- **Alcohols** have high bp due to hydrogen bonding
- **Carboxylic acids** have very high bp (strong H-bonding, can dimerize)
- **Alkanes** have low bp (only weak London forces)

**Order** (same carbon number): Carboxylic acid > Alcohol > Aldehyde/Ketone > Alkane

#### Solubility in Water

**General rule**: "Like dissolves like"

**Polar molecules**: Soluble in water (polar solvent)
- Alcohols (short chain)
- Carboxylic acids (short chain)
- Aldehydes and ketones (short chain)

**Nonpolar molecules**: Insoluble in water
- Alkanes
- Long-chain hydrocarbons
- Long-chain alcohols

**Trend**: As carbon chain increases, solubility in water decreases
- Methanol: Completely miscible with water
- Ethanol: Completely miscible with water
- Butanol: Slightly soluble
- Octanol: Nearly insoluble

### Polymers

**Definition**: Large molecules made of repeating units (monomers)

#### Addition Polymers

Formed by addition reactions of alkenes.

**Polyethylene** (polyethene):
- Monomer: Ethene \(CH_2=CH_2\)
- Polymer: \((-CH_2-CH_2-)_n\)
- Uses: Plastic bags, bottles

**Polypropylene** (polypropene):
- Monomer: Propene \(CH_2=CH-CH_3\)
- Polymer: \((-CH_2-CH(CH_3)-)_n\)
- Uses: Food containers, rope

**Polyvinyl chloride (PVC)**:
- Monomer: Chloroethene (vinyl chloride) \(CH_2=CHCl\)
- Polymer: \((-CH_2-CHCl-)_n\)
- Uses: Pipes, flooring, wire insulation

**Polystyrene**:
- Monomer: Styrene \(CH_2=CH-C_6H_5\)
- Uses: Foam cups, insulation, packaging

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Incorrect carbon chain numbering

**Wrong approach**: Numbering from the wrong end of the chain

**Why it's wrong**: IUPAC rules require giving substituents or functional groups the lowest possible numbers.

**Example**:
```
CH₃-CH₂-CH(CH₃)-CH₃
```
- Wrong: 3-methylbutane (numbering from left: 1-2-3-4)
- Correct: 2-methylbutane (numbering from right: 4-3-2-1 gives methyl position 2)

**How to avoid**: Number from both ends and choose the direction that gives the lowest numbers to substituents/functional groups.

### Mistake 2: Forgetting to indicate the position of functional groups

**Wrong approach**: Writing "butanol" or "butene" without specifying position

**Why it's wrong**: For molecules with 3+ carbons, the functional group position matters.

**Example**:
- Wrong: Butene (ambiguous - could be but-1-ene or but-2-ene)
- Correct: But-1-ene or But-2-ene (specifies where the double bond starts)

**How to avoid**: Always include the number indicating the position of the functional group, except when it's unambiguous (e.g., ethanol can only have -OH in one position).

### Mistake 3: Confusing structural formulas with molecular formulas

**Wrong approach**: Drawing \(C_4H_{10}\) as a single structure

**Why it's wrong**: \(C_4H_{10}\) is a molecular formula that represents multiple possible isomers.

**Example**:
- Molecular formula: \(C_4H_{10}\) (tells you the number of each atom)
- Possible structural isomers:
  - n-Butane: \(CH_3CH_2CH_2CH_3\)
  - 2-Methylpropane: \(CH_3CH(CH_3)CH_3\)

**How to avoid**: Remember that molecular formula shows composition, while structural formula shows arrangement. Always specify which isomer you mean.

### Mistake 4: Not recognizing functional group priority in naming

**Wrong approach**: Naming alcohols with -OH as substituents

**Why it's wrong**: Functional groups have priority order. Alcohols (-OH), aldehydes (-CHO), ketones (C=O), and carboxylic acids (-COOH) determine the suffix, not substituents.

**Priority order** (highest to lowest):
1. Carboxylic acid (-oic acid)
2. Ester (-oate)
3. Aldehyde (-al)
4. Ketone (-one)
5. Alcohol (-ol)
6. Amine (-amine)
7. Alkene (-ene)
8. Alkyne (-yne)
9. Alkane (-ane)

**Example**:
- A molecule with both -OH and C=C: The -OH takes priority
- Correct name: Prop-2-en-1-ol (not 3-hydroxypropene)

**How to avoid**: Learn the functional group priority order. The highest priority functional group determines the suffix.

### Mistake 5: Incorrect balancing of combustion equations

**Wrong approach**: Not balancing oxygen or water coefficients correctly

**Why it's wrong**: Combustion reactions must be balanced like any chemical equation.

**Example**:
Combustion of propane:
- Wrong: \(C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O\)
- Correct: \(C_3H_8 + 5O_2 \rightarrow 3CO_2 + 4H_2O\)

**Balancing strategy**:
1. Balance carbons first (3 carbons → 3 CO₂)
2. Balance hydrogens (8 H → 4 H₂O)
3. Balance oxygens last (3×2 + 4×1 = 10 O needed → 5 O₂)

**How to avoid**: Follow the systematic approach: C first, then H, then O last. Check your work by counting atoms on both sides.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Naming a branched alkane

**Problem**: Name the following compound:
```
      CH₃
       |
CH₃-CH-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃
```

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Find the longest continuous carbon chain
- Longest chain: 5 carbons (pentane)

**Step 2**: Identify substituents
- One methyl group (\(CH_3\))

**Step 3**: Number the chain
- From left: 1-2-3-4-5 → methyl on carbon 2
- From right: 5-4-3-2-1 → methyl on carbon 4
- Choose numbering that gives lowest number: **2**

**Step 4**: Write the name
- Position: 2
- Substituent: methyl
- Parent: pentane

**Answer**: 2-methylpentane

---

### Example 2: Determining molecular formula from name

**Problem**: Draw the structural formula for 2,3-dimethylbutane.

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Identify the parent chain
- "butane" = 4 carbons in main chain
- Draw: C-C-C-C

**Step 2**: Add substituents
- "2,3-dimethyl" = methyl groups on carbons 2 and 3
- "di" = two methyl groups

**Step 3**: Draw the structure:
```
    CH₃  CH₃
     |    |
CH₃-CH-CH-CH₃
     2   3
```

**Step 4**: Add hydrogens (each carbon needs 4 bonds total)
- Already shown above

**Step 5**: Verify molecular formula
- Count: 6 carbons, 14 hydrogens
- Formula: \(C_6H_{14}\)
- Check with alkane formula: \(C_nH_{2n+2} = C_6H_{2(6)+2} = C_6H_{14}\) ✓

**Answer**:
```
    CH₃  CH₃
     |    |
CH₃-CH-CH-CH₃
```

---

### Example 3: Balancing a combustion equation

**Problem**: Write and balance the equation for the complete combustion of butane (\(C_4H_{10}\)).

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Write the unbalanced equation
\[C_4H_{10} + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O\]

**Step 2**: Balance carbons
- 4 carbons in \(C_4H_{10}\) → need 4 \(CO_2\)
\[C_4H_{10} + O_2 \rightarrow 4CO_2 + H_2O\]

**Step 3**: Balance hydrogens
- 10 hydrogens in \(C_4H_{10}\) → need 5 \(H_2O\)
\[C_4H_{10} + O_2 \rightarrow 4CO_2 + 5H_2O\]

**Step 4**: Balance oxygens
- Right side: \(4(2) + 5(1) = 8 + 5 = 13\) oxygen atoms
- Need: \(\frac{13}{2} = 6.5\) \(O_2\) molecules

\[C_4H_{10} + 6.5O_2 \rightarrow 4CO_2 + 5H_2O\]

**Step 5**: Multiply all coefficients by 2 to get whole numbers
\[2C_4H_{10} + 13O_2 \rightarrow 8CO_2 + 10H_2O\]

**Answer**: \(2C_4H_{10} + 13O_2 \rightarrow 8CO_2 + 10H_2O\)

---

### Example 4: Identifying functional groups

**Problem**: Identify the functional groups in the following compound and name it:
\(CH_3CH_2COOH\)

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Identify the functional group
- Contains -COOH (carboxyl group)
- This is a carboxylic acid

**Step 2**: Count the carbons in the chain
- 3 carbons total (including the carbon in -COOH)
- 3 carbons = "propan-"

**Step 3**: Apply the suffix for carboxylic acid
- Suffix: "-oic acid"

**Step 4**: Combine
- Propan + oic acid = Propanoic acid

**Answer**: Functional group: Carboxylic acid; Name: Propanoic acid

---

### Example 5: Identifying isomers

**Problem**: Draw all structural isomers of \(C_5H_{12}\).

**Solution**:

**Step 1**: Understand what we're looking for
- Same molecular formula: \(C_5H_{12}\)
- Different structural arrangements

**Step 2**: Start with the straight chain
**Isomer 1**: Pentane (n-pentane)
```
CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃
```

**Step 3**: Create branched isomers with 4-carbon main chain
**Isomer 2**: 2-Methylbutane
```
      CH₃
       |
CH₃-CH-CH₂-CH₃
```

**Step 4**: Create branched isomers with 3-carbon main chain
**Isomer 3**: 2,2-Dimethylpropane
```
      CH₃
       |
CH₃-C-CH₃
       |
      CH₃
```

**Step 5**: Verify no more isomers possible
- Can't have a 2-carbon main chain with three methyl groups (would be unstable)
- All possible arrangements covered

**Answer**: There are 3 structural isomers of \(C_5H_{12}\):
1. Pentane
2. 2-Methylbutane
3. 2,2-Dimethylpropane

## SAT Chemistry Practice Problems

### Problem 1
Which of the following molecular formulas represents an alkene?

(A) \(C_3H_8\)
(B) \(C_4H_{10}\)
(C) \(C_5H_{10}\)
(D) \(C_6H_{14}\)
(E) \(C_7H_{16}\)

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: C** (\(C_5H_{10}\))

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    Alkenes have the general formula \(C_nH_{2n}\).

    Check each option:
    - **(A)** \(C_3H_8\): Is this \(C_3H_{2(3)}\)? → \(C_3H_6\)? No, it's \(C_3H_8\) (alkane: \(C_nH_{2n+2}\))
    - **(B)** \(C_4H_{10}\): Is this \(C_4H_{2(4)}\)? → \(C_4H_8\)? No, it's \(C_4H_{10}\) (alkane)
    - **(C)** \(C_5H_{10}\): Is this \(C_5H_{2(5)}\)? → \(C_5H_{10}\)? **Yes!** ✓ (alkene)
    - **(D)** \(C_6H_{14}\): Is this \(C_6H_{2(6)}\)? → \(C_6H_{12}\)? No, it's \(C_6H_{14}\) (alkane)
    - **(E)** \(C_7H_{16}\): Is this \(C_7H_{2(7)}\)? → \(C_7H_{14}\)? No, it's \(C_7H_{16}\) (alkane)

    **Key concept**: Alkenes follow \(C_nH_{2n}\), alkanes follow \(C_nH_{2n+2}\), alkynes follow \(C_nH_{2n-2}\).

### Problem 2
What is the IUPAC name for the following compound?
```
CH₃-CH₂-CH(CH₃)-CH₃
```

(A) Butane
(B) 2-Methylbutane
(C) 2-Methylpropane
(D) 3-Methylbutane
(E) Pentane

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: B (2-Methylbutane)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    **Step 1**: Find the longest continuous carbon chain
    - Count the main chain: CH₃-CH₂-CH-CH₃ = 4 carbons
    - Parent name: Butane

    **Step 2**: Identify substituents
    - One CH₃ branch = Methyl group

    **Step 3**: Number the chain to give substituent the lowest number
    - From left: 1-2-3-4 → methyl on position 3
    - From right: 4-3-2-1 → methyl on position 2
    - Choose position 2 (lower)

    **Step 4**: Combine the name
    - 2-Methylbutane

    **Why not other options?**
    - (A) Butane: Ignores the methyl branch
    - (C) 2-Methylpropane: Wrong parent chain (should be 4 carbons, not 3)
    - (D) 3-Methylbutane: Wrong numbering (should be position 2)
    - (E) Pentane: This would be a 5-carbon straight chain

### Problem 3
Which functional group is present in ethanol (\(CH_3CH_2OH\))?

(A) Aldehyde
(B) Ketone
(C) Carboxylic acid
(D) Alcohol
(E) Ester

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: D (Alcohol)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    Identify the functional group in \(CH_3CH_2OH\):
    - Contains -OH group bonded to a carbon chain
    - This is the hydroxyl group, characteristic of alcohols

    **Why not other options?**
    - (A) Aldehyde: Would have -CHO group (e.g., \(CH_3CHO\))
    - (B) Ketone: Would have C=O in the middle of chain (e.g., \(CH_3COCH_3\))
    - (C) Carboxylic acid: Would have -COOH group (e.g., \(CH_3COOH\))
    - (E) Ester: Would have -COO- group (e.g., \(CH_3COOCH_3\))

    **Key concept**: The -OH group defines alcohols.

### Problem 4
How many structural isomers exist for \(C_4H_{10}\)?

(A) 1
(B) 2
(C) 3
(D) 4
(E) 5

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: B (2 isomers)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    Draw all possible structures with formula \(C_4H_{10}\):

    **Isomer 1**: n-Butane (straight chain)
    ```
    CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃
    ```

    **Isomer 2**: 2-Methylpropane (branched)
    ```
          CH₃
           |
    CH₃-CH-CH₃
    ```

    **Check**: Can we make more arrangements?
    - A 2-carbon chain with two ethyl groups? No, that would be butane (already counted)
    - Any other branching? No, only 4 carbons total limits the possibilities

    **Answer**: Only 2 structural isomers possible for \(C_4H_{10}\).

### Problem 5
What type of reaction occurs when ethene (\(C_2H_4\)) reacts with bromine (\(Br_2\)) to form \(C_2H_4Br_2\)?

(A) Combustion
(B) Substitution
(C) Addition
(D) Elimination
(E) Decomposition

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: C (Addition)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    **Reaction**: \(C_2H_4 + Br_2 \rightarrow C_2H_4Br_2\)

    **Analysis**:
    - **Reactants**: Two molecules (ethene + bromine)
    - **Product**: One molecule (dibromoethane)
    - The C=C double bond in ethene is broken
    - Bromine atoms are added to the carbon atoms

    **This is an addition reaction**: Two molecules combine into one by breaking a multiple bond.

    **Why not other options?**
    - (A) Combustion: Would produce CO₂ and H₂O
    - (B) Substitution: Would replace an atom (e.g., H replaced by Br), formula would be \(C_2H_3Br + HBr\)
    - (D) Elimination: Would remove atoms to form a double bond (opposite direction)
    - (E) Decomposition: Would break down one molecule into smaller pieces

    **Key concept**: Addition reactions add atoms across a double or triple bond, forming a single bond.

### Problem 6
Which of the following compounds has the highest boiling point?

(A) Methane (\(CH_4\))
(B) Ethane (\(C_2H_6\))
(C) Propane (\(C_3H_8\))
(D) Butane (\(C_4H_{10}\))
(E) Pentane (\(C_5H_{12}\))

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: E (Pentane)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    All compounds are alkanes with only London dispersion forces (weak intermolecular forces).

    **Trend**: As molecular size/chain length increases, boiling point increases
    - Larger molecules have more electrons
    - More surface area for intermolecular attractions
    - Stronger London dispersion forces

    **Order by increasing boiling point**:
    Methane < Ethane < Propane < Butane < Pentane

    **Actual boiling points**:
    - Methane: -162°C
    - Ethane: -89°C
    - Propane: -42°C
    - Butane: -0.5°C
    - Pentane: 36°C

    **Answer**: Pentane has the highest boiling point.

### Problem 7
Complete combustion of an alkane produces:

(A) Carbon monoxide and water
(B) Carbon dioxide and hydrogen
(C) Carbon dioxide and water
(D) Carbon and water
(E) Carbon monoxide and hydrogen

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: C (Carbon dioxide and water)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    **Complete combustion** means sufficient oxygen is present for all carbon to be fully oxidized.

    **General equation**:
    \[\text{Hydrocarbon} + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O\]

    **Example**:
    \[CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O\]

    **Products**:
    - Carbon atoms → Carbon dioxide (\(CO_2\))
    - Hydrogen atoms → Water (\(H_2O\))

    **Why not other options?**
    - (A) CO: This is incomplete combustion (insufficient oxygen)
    - (B) H₂: Combustion oxidizes hydrogen to H₂O, not free H₂
    - (D) C: This is incomplete combustion (very limited oxygen)
    - (E) CO and H₂: These are incomplete combustion products

    **Key concept**: Complete combustion always produces CO₂ and H₂O. Incomplete combustion produces CO or C (soot).

### Problem 8
What is the product when ethanol undergoes dehydration?

(A) Ethane
(B) Ethene
(C) Ethyne
(D) Ethanoic acid
(E) Ethanal

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: B (Ethene)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    **Dehydration** means removal of water (H₂O).

    **Reaction**:
    \[C_2H_5OH \xrightarrow{\text{conc. } H_2SO_4, \text{ heat}} C_2H_4 + H_2O\]
    \[\text{Ethanol} \rightarrow \text{Ethene} + \text{Water}\]

    **Mechanism**:
    - Remove H from one carbon and OH from adjacent carbon
    - Forms a C=C double bond
    - This is an elimination reaction

    **Structure**:
    - Ethanol: \(CH_3-CH_2-OH\)
    - Remove H₂O → \(CH_2=CH_2\) (ethene)

    **Why not other options?**
    - (A) Ethane: Would require hydrogenation (adding H₂), not dehydration
    - (C) Ethyne: Would require double dehydration (two C≡C bond, not formed this way)
    - (D) Ethanoic acid: Would require oxidation, not dehydration
    - (E) Ethanal: Would require oxidation, not dehydration

### Problem 9
Which of the following is a tertiary alcohol?

(A) \(CH_3OH\)
(B) \(CH_3CH_2OH\)
(C) \(CH_3CH(OH)CH_3\)
(D) \((CH_3)_3COH\)
(E) \(CH_3CH_2CH_2OH\)

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: D** (\((CH_3)_3COH\))

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    **Definition of alcohol types**:
    - **Primary (1°)**: -OH carbon bonded to ONE other carbon
    - **Secondary (2°)**: -OH carbon bonded to TWO other carbons
    - **Tertiary (3°)**: -OH carbon bonded to THREE other carbons

    **Analyze each option**:

    **(A)** \(CH_3OH\): Methanol
    - OH carbon bonded to zero other carbons
    - Primary (special case)

    **(B)** \(CH_3CH_2OH\): Ethanol
    - OH carbon bonded to one other carbon (CH₃)
    - Primary (1°)

    **(C)** \(CH_3CH(OH)CH_3\): Propan-2-ol
    - OH carbon bonded to two other carbons (two CH₃ groups)
    - Secondary (2°)

    **(D)** \((CH_3)_3COH\): 2-Methylpropan-2-ol
    ```
          CH₃
           |
    CH₃-C-OH
           |
          CH₃
    ```
    - OH carbon bonded to THREE other carbons (three CH₃ groups)
    - **Tertiary (3°)** ✓

    **(E)** \(CH_3CH_2CH_2OH\): Propan-1-ol
    - OH carbon bonded to one other carbon (CH₂)
    - Primary (1°)

### Problem 10
An ester is formed by the reaction between:

(A) Two alcohols
(B) An alcohol and a ketone
(C) An alcohol and a carboxylic acid
(D) Two carboxylic acids
(E) An alcohol and an aldehyde

??? note "Click to reveal solution"
    **Answer: C (An alcohol and a carboxylic acid)**

    **Step-by-step solution**:

    **Esterification reaction**:
    \[\text{Carboxylic acid} + \text{Alcohol} \xrightarrow{H^+ \text{ catalyst}} \text{Ester} + \text{Water}\]

    **Example**:
    \[CH_3COOH + C_2H_5OH \rightleftharpoons CH_3COOC_2H_5 + H_2O\]
    \[\text{Ethanoic acid} + \text{Ethanol} \rightleftharpoons \text{Ethyl ethanoate} + \text{Water}\]

    **Ester structure**: \(R-COO-R'\)
    - The \(R-COO-\) part comes from the carboxylic acid
    - The \(-R'\) part comes from the alcohol

    **Conditions**: Acid catalyst (conc. H₂SO₄) and heat

    **Why not other options?**
    - (A) Two alcohols: Would form an ether (R-O-R')
    - (B) Alcohol + ketone: Can form hemiketal/ketal (not a simple ester)
    - (D) Two carboxylic acids: Would form an acid anhydride
    - (E) Alcohol + aldehyde: Can form hemiacetal/acetal (not a simple ester)

## Step Checker: Verify Your Work

Use these checklists to verify your organic chemistry work.

### Naming Organic Compounds Checker

**Step-by-step checklist**:
- [ ] **Step 1**: Identify the longest continuous carbon chain
- [ ] **Step 2**: Determine the base name from the number of carbons
  - 1C = meth-, 2C = eth-, 3C = prop-, 4C = but-, 5C = pent-, etc.
- [ ] **Step 3**: Identify any functional groups present
- [ ] **Step 4**: Determine the suffix based on functional group priority
  - Carboxylic acid > Aldehyde > Ketone > Alcohol > Alkene > Alkyne > Alkane
- [ ] **Step 5**: Identify and name all substituents (branches)
- [ ] **Step 6**: Number the carbon chain from the end that gives the functional group/substituents the lowest numbers
- [ ] **Step 7**: Write substituents in alphabetical order (ignore di-, tri-, etc.)
- [ ] **Step 8**: Include position numbers for functional groups and substituents
- [ ] **Step 9**: Combine: (position)-(substituent)-(position)-(functional group)-(parent name)-(suffix)
- [ ] **Step 10**: Check that the name is unambiguous and follows IUPAC rules

### Balancing Combustion Equations Checker

**Step-by-step checklist**:
- [ ] **Step 1**: Write the unbalanced equation: Hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
- [ ] **Step 2**: Balance carbons first (coefficient in front of CO₂)
- [ ] **Step 3**: Balance hydrogens second (coefficient in front of H₂O)
- [ ] **Step 4**: Count oxygen atoms on the right side
- [ ] **Step 5**: Determine O₂ coefficient needed (divide total O by 2)
- [ ] **Step 6**: If O₂ coefficient is a fraction, multiply all coefficients by 2
- [ ] **Step 7**: Verify the equation is balanced (count atoms on both sides)
- [ ] **Step 8**: Check that all coefficients are whole numbers and in lowest terms

### Drawing Structural Formulas Checker

**Step-by-step checklist**:
- [ ] **Step 1**: Determine the carbon chain length from the parent name
- [ ] **Step 2**: Draw the main carbon chain
- [ ] **Step 3**: Number the carbon atoms
- [ ] **Step 4**: Add functional groups at specified positions
- [ ] **Step 5**: Add substituents (branches) at specified positions
- [ ] **Step 6**: Add hydrogens to satisfy carbon's valence of 4
  - Each carbon must have 4 bonds total
- [ ] **Step 7**: Verify the molecular formula matches expected formula
  - Alkanes: \(C_nH_{2n+2}\), Alkenes: \(C_nH_{2n}\), Alkynes: \(C_nH_{2n-2}\)
- [ ] **Step 8**: Check that the structure matches the name given
- [ ] **Step 9**: Confirm no carbon has more than 4 bonds

### Identifying Isomers Checker

**Step-by-step checklist**:
- [ ] **Step 1**: Write down the molecular formula
- [ ] **Step 2**: Start with the longest possible carbon chain (straight chain)
- [ ] **Step 3**: Systematically shorten the main chain by one carbon and add as branches
- [ ] **Step 4**: For each chain length, try all possible positions for substituents
- [ ] **Step 5**: Draw each unique structure
- [ ] **Step 6**: Name each structure to ensure they're truly different
- [ ] **Step 7**: Check that each isomer has the same molecular formula
- [ ] **Step 8**: Verify no duplicate structures (same structure drawn differently)
- [ ] **Step 9**: Consider functional group isomers (same formula, different functional groups)
- [ ] **Step 10**: For alkenes, check for geometric (cis-trans) isomers
